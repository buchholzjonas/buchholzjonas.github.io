Regarding the Cōḻa reference, Āl. refers to Kaliṅkattupparaṇi 178, which describes how the Cōḻa king Karikālaṉ inscribed his royal symbol on the Himalayas.

As it does not bow to anyone except for ruby-coloured Ekāmranātha, who is similar to it because he makes everything that joins him have his nature (because it makes everything that joins it have its colour),*The point of the comparison seems to be that Mount Meru's golden colour makes anything that stands next to it also appear golden, whereas Śiva makes the liberated souls partake in his nature once (the word வண்ணம்vaṇṇam thus has to be understood in the double sense of "colour" and "nature"). Or does the comparison refer to Śiva's "ruby colour" (மாணிக்க வண்ணர்māṇikka vaṇṇar)? that golden mountain stands still, dividing the eight agreeable great directions and creating day and night as the many great planets and asterisms circumambulate it every day.*Mount Meru is considered the centre of the universe and thus the origin of the cardinal directions as well as the focal point around which the celestial bodies revolve. Because the sun revolves around Mount Meru, it is responsible for the existence of day and night (விளக்குviḷakku here has to be understood as "to create", not in the usual meaning "to illuminate").
